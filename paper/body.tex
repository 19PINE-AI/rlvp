\section{Introduction}

The recipe that produced the recent generation of reasoning models is disarmingly simple: sample a group of trajectories, reward the ones that solve the task, and push the policy toward them with a group-relative advantage. DeepSeek-R1-Zero showed that this outcome-only signal, applied to a base model with no supervised cold-start, is enough to grow sophisticated reasoning behavior~\citep{deepseekr1}. The agentic era inherited the recipe wholesale---train a tool-using agent by rewarding the trajectories that complete the task, and nothing else.

But an agentic trajectory is not a chain of thought. It is a sequence of \emph{interactions with an environment}, and the environment is itself a \emph{verifier}: error codes, tool results, state transitions, and pre/post-conditions are all machine-checkable intermediate signal. Reasoning RL had no such signal---you cannot verify a reasoning step mid-stream without redoing it---so outcome-only reward was not a choice but the only option. Agentic RL does have it, and throws it away. This is wasteful for two distinct reasons. First, a \emph{gradient} reason: group-relative methods such as GRPO estimate advantage by centering reward on the group mean~\citep{deepseekmath}, so when \emph{every} rollout in a group fails---the common case early in training on a hard task---the advantages are all zero and the update is dead. The outcome signal is silent exactly when the policy most needs shaping. Second, a \emph{safety} reason: the outcome reward is defined on the terminal state, so it is blind to harm that happens \emph{on the path}---an agent that deletes a production database and then rebuilds it ends in a correct state and is rewarded, even though the irreversible damage already occurred.

The intermediate signal the environment provides could fill both gaps. The trouble is that using it naively backfires, in ways that have made practitioners wary of process rewards altogether. A penalty an agent can satisfy by \emph{doing nothing}---``never call before looking up the account''---is maximized by an idle policy, and in the all-fail regime, where the process channel is the only gradient, the optimizer climbs straight into that degenerate \emph{compliance} optimum and task success collapses to zero. And a \emph{learned} progress critic, the natural way to get a dense signal without writing rules, simply moves the reward-hacking up one level: the policy learns to fool the critic. Process rewards sometimes give large gains and sometimes cause catastrophic collapse, and the field has lacked a way to tell the two cases apart in advance.

\paragraph{This paper.} We give that way. We argue that whether a dense process reward helps is not a property of the algorithm but of the \emph{domain}, and we make it precise with a single criterion (Figure~\ref{fig:criterion}):

\begin{quote}
\emph{A dense process reward improves agentic RL if and only if it is an \textbf{un-gameable, verifiable proxy for progress}: a potential strictly finer than the terminal outcome (so it varies across the failed rollouts in an all-fail group), whose cheapest maximizer must still solve the task (so the optimizer cannot game it), and whose intermediate values the policy can \textbf{actually reach} (so the variation is realized, not merely possible).}
\end{quote}

The three conditions---finer-than-outcome, un-gameable, reachable---are each individually testable \emph{before} training, by inspecting the domain and a handful of base-policy rollouts. The criterion is grounded in potential-based reward shaping, which guarantees that any potential leaves the optimal policy unchanged~\citep{ng1999}: a process reward is therefore safe to add exactly when it is such a potential, and useful exactly when it additionally supplies group-relative gradient the outcome cannot.

\paragraph{Validation as a predictive law.} We test the criterion not by proposing one method and showing it works, but by treating our entire body of results---successes \emph{and} failures---as predictions the criterion must get right. Across five settings (Table~\ref{tab:settings}), the criterion's verdict matches the measured outcome in every case. Where it says \emph{yes}: in theorem proving a kernel-verified progress signal turns all-fail groups into gradient and eliminates dead updates; in system administration an un-gameable harm penalty cuts destructive actions roughly fourfold at \emph{equal} task success. Where it says \emph{no}: in customer-service workflows the residual difficulty is task \emph{intent}, which is the reward itself and not any verifiable rule, so process rewards neutralize harm but never beat outcome-only; in software repair a finer potential exists for only a third of problems and is empirically \emph{unreachable}, so it supplies no gradient. A controlled sweep that walks one signal from aligned to misaligned then reproduces the criterion's verdict arm by arm within a single experiment.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{A criterion} (\S\ref{sec:criterion}) that predicts, before training, whether a dense process reward will help in a given domain---unifying the all-fail-group blindness of group-relative RL, the compliance attractor of misaligned penalties, and the failure of learned critics under one condition: an un-gameable, reachable, verifiable potential finer than the outcome.
\item \textbf{A controlled demonstration that the criterion is predictive} (\S\ref{sec:law}): an un-gameability sweep at 30B walks a single Lean signal from aligned to misaligned and the policy's fate tracks admissibility---penalty-bearing signals collapse, penalty-free progress signals survive, and gating a gameable signal on the outcome restores it; a granularity/sparsity study shows the gain scales with the potential's fineness; and an SWE reachability probe shows a structurally-finer potential that is empirically vacuous.
\item \textbf{Two positive instantiations} where the criterion says yes: process rewards make outcome learning \emph{sample-efficient} by converting dead updates into gradient (\S\ref{sec:efficiency}--\ref{sec:anatomy}), and an un-gameable penalty achieves \emph{harm reduction on an axis the outcome cannot see} (\S\ref{sec:harm}).
\item \textbf{Two boundaries} where it says no, mapped honestly: a discovery ceiling where no on-policy reward escapes a never-sampled precondition (\S\ref{sec:ceiling}); and an intent ceiling on a real benchmark where verifiable rules cover policy \emph{validity} but not task \emph{intent} (\S\ref{sec:boundary}), with a learned self-critic shown to relocate rather than remove the problem (\S\ref{sec:selfcritique}).
\end{itemize}

\section{A Criterion for When Process Rewards Help}
\label{sec:criterion}

\paragraph{The gradient gap (why one would want a process reward).} Group-relative policy optimization centers each trajectory's reward on its group mean. When rewards vary within a group the centering is informative; when \emph{every} rollout fails, the rewards are identical, every advantage is exactly zero, and the group yields no gradient---a \emph{dead update} (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}). On short-horizon tasks a competent base model makes all-fail groups rare. On long-horizon agentic tasks the base success rate is low, all-fail groups dominate early training, and outcome-only RL is starved of signal during exactly the phase that determines whether it ever gets off the ground. A process reward that depends on \emph{how} an episode behaves rather than \emph{whether} it succeeds is non-zero on those very groups---if it varies across the failed rollouts, it is gradient where the outcome has none.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_mechanism.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Why a process signal can help where the outcome cannot.} On a hard task most early groups are \emph{all-fail}. \emph{Left:} outcome-only GRPO sees identical (zero) rewards, so the group-mean-centered advantage is zero on every token---a dead update. \emph{Right:} a process signal attached to individual tool calls differs across the failed episodes (one read before writing, another did not), so the update is non-zero. The condition for this to work is that the process signal \emph{varies within the group}---i.e.\ it is a potential strictly finer than the outcome.}
\label{fig:mechanism}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{The two failure modes (why one cannot use just any process reward).} The same all-fail regime that makes a process reward valuable also makes it dangerous, because the process channel is then the \emph{only} gradient. If the cheapest way to maximize the process reward is something other than solving the task, the optimizer will find it. Two cases recur. A \textbf{penalty} is almost always gameable: its cheapest maximizer is inaction (never act $\Rightarrow$ never violate), so on all-fail batches it drives the policy into a degenerate compliance-only optimum---success goes to zero while ``compliance'' is perfect. A \textbf{learned critic} is gameable in a subtler way: defining progress by a learned proxy relocates the credit-assignment problem into the proxy, which the policy then learns to fool. Neither failure is about tuning; both are structural.

\paragraph{The criterion.} These observations have a common root. A process reward is admissible as a learning signal precisely when it is an \textbf{un-gameable verifiable potential finer than the outcome}, and it is \emph{useful} when, in addition, the policy reaches its intermediate values. Concretely, three conditions, each checkable before training:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt,label=(\arabic*)]
\item \textbf{Finer than outcome.} The signal $\Phi$ takes more values than the binary terminal reward, and those values \emph{vary across the rollouts in an all-fail group}. (A uniform $\Phi$ is centered out by GRPO and supplies no gradient.)
\item \textbf{Un-gameable.} The cheapest policy that maximizes $\Phi$ must still solve the task. Operationally: name the cheapest policy that maximizes $\Phi$ \emph{without} solving the task; if it is degenerate (idle, padding, error-avoidance by inaction), $\Phi$ is inadmissible.
\item \textbf{Reachable.} The policy's rollouts actually attain intermediate $\Phi$ values---there is realized within-group variance, not merely a potential that exists on paper.
\end{enumerate}
Potential-based shaping~\citep{ng1999} supplies the theoretical backstop: a reward of the form $\gamma\Phi(s')-\Phi(s)$ leaves the optimal policy unchanged for \emph{any} $\Phi$, so a verifiable potential is always \emph{safe} to add; the criterion says when it is also \emph{helpful}. A verifiable progress credit (Lean's goal count strictly decreasing, a failing test turning green) can satisfy all three conditions; a bare penalty fails (2); a learned critic fails verifiability and usually (2); and a domain whose only finer signal is unreachable fails (3). Table~\ref{tab:settings} previews how the five settings we study fall out.

\section{The Criterion as a Predictive Law}
\label{sec:law}

If the criterion is more than a restatement of intuition, it should make \emph{advance predictions} that a controlled experiment confirms. We provide three such tests.

\paragraph{An un-gameability sweep: admissibility predicts survival.} We construct five process signals for Lean theorem proving on miniF2F~\citep{minif2f}, spanning aligned to misaligned, and pre-register each one's cheapest gaming policy and the criterion's verdict. We then train Qwen3-30B-A3B~\citep{qwen3} with each, holding everything else fixed (Figure~\ref{fig:sweep}). The result tracks admissibility arm by arm. The two signals carrying a \emph{misaligned penalty}---an errored-tactic penalty alone, and a progress credit \emph{plus} that penalty---both collapse to zero and stay there: the penalty's cheapest maximizer (avoid errors by not attempting) is the compliance attractor, and it is lethal even when a useful progress credit is also present. The two \emph{penalty-free} signals survive: an aligned goal-progress discharge, and even a \emph{gameable} ``any-valid-tactic'' credit, stay productive. And gating that gameable credit on the eventual outcome---paying it only in episodes that succeed, which makes it un-gameable by construction---turns the worst-behaved survivor into the best arm. Penalty kills; gating rescues; the criterion called both in advance.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_ungameability_sweep.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The criterion predicts which signals survive (Qwen3-30B, miniF2F).} \emph{(a)} Success trajectories for five process signals walked from aligned to misaligned. The two carrying a misaligned penalty (dashed) fall into the dead zone and stay; the penalty-free signals (solid) survive the high single-run variance. \emph{(b)} Terminal success (mean of the last three iterations): both penalty-bearing arms are dead, while \emph{outcome-gating} a gameable discharge yields the best arm. This is high-variance, single-seed at 30B; we read the \emph{ordering and survival}, not the precise values (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).}
\label{fig:sweep}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Granularity and sparsity: the gain scales with the potential's fineness.} A complementary, lower-variance test isolates condition (1). On a synthetic $N$-stage chained task with a verifiable potential equal to the number of completed stages, we vary the potential's \emph{granularity} (coarse $=$ outcome, versus one milestone, versus every stage) and the outcome's \emph{sparsity} ($N$). A potential strictly finer than the outcome drives success where the outcome alone is essentially zero, and the advantage \emph{grows} as the outcome becomes sparser---exactly the regime where all-fail groups dominate and the finer potential has the most uniquely-available gradient to offer. This is the positive, controlled face of the criterion: fineness is not incidental, it is the mechanism.

\paragraph{A reachability probe: a finer potential that is vacuous.} Conditions (1) and (2) are about the signal; condition (3) is about the \emph{policy}, and it is the one that quietly fails in hard domains. We test it directly on SWE-bench software repair~\citep{swebench}, where the natural potential is the fraction of the hidden \texttt{FAIL\_TO\_PASS} tests an episode makes pass. Two facts emerge (Figure~\ref{fig:reachability}). Structurally, the finer potential barely exists: two-thirds of instances have a single failing test, so $\Phi$ collapses to the binary outcome; only a third are ``multi-test'' with any room for partial progress. Empirically, even that third is \emph{unreachable}: across 156 rollouts of a 30B policy, \emph{every} episode scores $\Phi=0$---the model never makes a single hidden test pass, let alone a proper subset. The within-group variance is exactly zero, so a $\Phi$-based reward would be centered out to no gradient. This is why the dense SWE signal fails, and the criterion locates the cause precisely: not gameability, but \emph{unreachability}---there is no realized intermediate state for any reward, gameable or not, to act on.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_ec_reachability.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{In software repair the finer potential is structurally rare and empirically unreachable.} \emph{(a)} Of 48 single-file-fix SWE-bench instances, two-thirds have a single failing test, so the test-fraction potential equals the binary outcome; only one-third admit a finer $\Phi$. \emph{(b)} For those, a 30B policy's rollouts never realize it: all 156 rollouts (multi- and single-test) score $\Phi=0$. The shaded band of intermediate values exists but is empty---zero within-group variance, hence zero gradient. The criterion's third gate, reachability, is what fails here.}

\section{Where the Criterion Says Yes, I: Sample-Efficiency in Theorem Proving}
\label{sec:efficiency}

When the verifiable signal \emph{is} the bottleneck to success---read before write, decrease the goal count, pass the gate---the process channel and the outcome point the same way, and the criterion predicts a large efficiency gain. We confirm it on a controlled suite of long-horizon agentic tasks, where every claim about gradient is exact rather than estimated. The testbed is a family of \emph{$N$-stage chained tasks}: an agent completes $N$ independent file-operations subtasks in one episode using tools \texttt{list\_dir}, \texttt{read\_file}, \texttt{write\_file}, \texttt{delete}, \texttt{run\_tests}, \texttt{submit}; success requires all $N$ stages, so $N$ tunes base difficulty smoothly ($N{=}4$ sits near $8\%$ base success). We instantiate the process signal as \textbf{Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Penalties} (RLVP): a penalty when a call violates a verifiable rule (overwrite a file never read; submit without testing a change) and a credit when a call \emph{discharges} a pending obligation (reads before writing, tests after mutating). These are \emph{specifications}, written once, requiring no solved trajectories---the analog of R1-Zero's verifier, not of a supervised cold-start. The credit is carried on a separate channel with its own normalizer and attached to the \emph{responsible tokens}; all experiments train Qwen3-4B~\citep{qwen3} with our own GRPO, and efficiency is always counted in \emph{episodes generated} so that resampling costs are visible.

\paragraph{Dead updates are not a rounding error, and the fix is causal.} We sample a batch of rollouts \emph{once} and score it under both credit schemes (Figure~\ref{fig:paired}). Outcome-only credit produces a dead, zero-gradient update on one in four identical batches; the process credit is never dead, because it produces gradient from the same failed episodes the outcome channel reduces to zero. Same rollouts, opposite outcomes: the effect is the credit scheme, not the data.

\label{fig:paired}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{The gain is large and consistent; DAPO's is neither.} Figure~\ref{fig:efficiency} plots held-out success against episodes generated. Outcome-only GRPO crawls---it spends most of its budget on dead updates---then converges only at the end; RLVP rises immediately by learning from the early all-fail groups. Reimplemented dense-process baselines (GiGPO-style step advantages~\citep{gigpo}, StepTool-style per-call rewards~\citep{steptool}) also far outpace outcome-only: the broad lesson is that \emph{any} admissible dense signal helps. Aggregated over three seeds (Figure~\ref{fig:seeds}), RLVP reaches the success threshold several times faster than GRPO with \emph{near-zero} seed variance, while outcome-only GRPO is a lottery governed by when it first stumbles into a non-all-fail group. DAPO~\citep{dapo}, designed for the same all-fail problem, \emph{discards} such groups and resamples; we measure a $5.6\times$ sampling tax that leaves it, on the fair axis of episodes generated, slower than vanilla GRPO. Confronted with an all-fail group, DAPO says ``no information, discard''; RLVP says ``no \emph{outcome} information, but plenty of \emph{process} information.'' Breaking the binary-reward degeneracy is exactly what lets RLVP keep the failures DAPO must throw away.

\section{An Anatomy of the Gain}
\label{sec:anatomy}

Which component does the work? Ablating on $N{=}4$ (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}) revises some intuitions. \textbf{The token-attached channel is the efficiency lever}: folding the same rule rewards into the scalar return doubles the episodes to threshold---placing credit on the responsible tokens, not merely having a dense reward, is what buys the speed, and is the operational reason per-channel (not global) normalization is non-negotiable. \textbf{Annealing protects the ceiling}: removing it does not slow learning but lowers final success, because unrelaxed process pressure drifts the policy toward over-compliant, bloated episodes---a mild version of the compliance attractor that returns with force in \S\ref{sec:boundary}. \textbf{The discharge credit is the positive signal that escapes all-fail groups}: penalties suppress wrong moves but cannot induce a never-tried right one, and removing the discharge leaves more dead iterations and a lower ceiling. Finally, \textbf{the signal can be free}: replacing every hand-written rule with three \emph{universal} meta-rules derived only from per-tool category tags (observe/mutate/verify/terminal) and the environment's own error codes recovers the hand-engineered efficiency almost exactly, with no task-specific human input---RLVP's specification cost can, in well-structured environments, be paid by metadata that tool schemas already expose.

A per-step length cost, helpful only on very long horizons, hurts here.}

\section{Where the Criterion Says Yes, II: Harm Reduction Independent of Outcome}
\label{sec:harm}

The second place the criterion says yes is the safety use the outcome reward structurally cannot serve. Because the terminal reward is defined on the final state, it is blind to \emph{irreversible harm on the path}; a verifiable penalty on harmful actions is exactly an un-gameable bound on that path (its cheapest maximizer---take the harmful action zero times---\emph{is} the desired behavior, so it does not invite the compliance attractor the way an orthogonal procedural penalty does). We test this on real TerminalBench Docker tasks with Qwen3-4B, with penalties on repeat-error and blind-destructive actions (Table~\ref{tab:harm}). At this scale the model rarely solves the benchmark, so \emph{task success is identical and at the floor}---and yet RLVP commits roughly \emph{four times fewer} harmful actions. Crucially it does not achieve this by going passive: it takes about three times \emph{more} productive actions while violating less. This is the clean separation the criterion predicts---harm lives on an axis independent of the outcome, and an un-gameable penalty reduces it without changing whether the task is solved. (Operationally: you cannot delete the production database and then ``finish'' the repair---the harm already happened, regardless of the final state.)

\section{The Discovery Ceiling of Self-Evolution}
\label{sec:ceiling}

Sample efficiency is a statement about \emph{speed} to a ceiling the chained tasks eventually reach. To ask whether a process reward can raise the \emph{ceiling}, we need a task the model cannot saturate, and one that is hard to \emph{discover} rather than hard to \emph{compute} (RLVP guides procedure, not answers). We build a \emph{silent precondition gate}: to write a protected file the agent must first read an access-control file and request access; skip either and the write silently no-ops---it reports success but does nothing, and the task fails with no hint why. Calibration confirms the trap is total: the base model solves it $0\%$ of the time and never reads the access file, even when the rule is in its prompt. The result (Figure~\ref{fig:gated}) is sharper than ``RLVP wins'': \emph{every} reward-only method---outcome, DAPO, clean RLVP---fails to zero, because the discharge credit for reading the access file can only reward that behavior if the policy \emph{samples} it, and it never does. A single rule-synthesized demonstration, injected through the process channel, breaks the wall and drives the policy to perfect, generalizing success where prompting the same behavior had failed. This is the criterion's condition (3)---reachability---in its starkest form: when the required behavior is never sampled, no on-policy reward can induce it, and imitation must seed what reward then grows. It maps cleanly onto R1-Zero (discoverable workflow: reward-only suffices) versus R1 (un-discoverable precondition: a demonstration cold-start is necessary).

\section{Where the Criterion Says No: Task Intent Is Not a Verifiable Rule}
\label{sec:boundary}

The hardest case for any process-reward method is a task whose difficulty is not procedural at all. We move onto $\tau$-bench-style airline customer service~\citep{taubench}, whose reward turns on \emph{semantic} policy adherence---authorization, refund eligibility, confirmation---rather than the structural ordering generic rules encode. The criterion predicts the first gate will fail: there is no verifiable potential finer than the outcome, because what is missing is \emph{intent}, which is the reward itself. The experiment (Figure~\ref{fig:tau2}) confirms it in three informative tiers. \emph{Generic} structural rules---the read-before-write hygiene that works on chained tasks---are now orthogonal to the reward and actively \emph{harm}: the policy discovers that the cheapest way never to violate ``confirm before calling'' is to not place the risky calls at all, and collapses into the compliance attractor within a handful of iterations. \emph{Policy-derived procedural} rules (obtain the user id and look up the reservation before modifying it), outcome-instrumental by construction and paired with early annealing, remove the harm---a correct modification \emph{requires} the looked-up state, so the discharge pulls toward the productive workflow rather than idleness. \emph{Verifiable semantic} rules (do not modify a basic-economy reservation; do not use a payment method absent from the profile) prune failure-bound actions and lift the best iterations nearly to outcome-only's peak. But neither aligned tier \emph{beats} outcome-only on average. The coverage gradient saturates: verifiable rules express policy \emph{validity}---whether a modification is permissible---while the residual difficulty is \emph{which} permissible change \emph{this} user wants, and a rule encoding that would simply be the task specification. RLVP accelerates the policy-verifiable component of success and neutralizes harm, but cannot substitute for outcome learning of intent (Figure~\ref{fig:boundary}).

\section{A Learned Critic Relocates the Hack, It Does Not Remove It}
\label{sec:selfcritique}

If verifiable rules cannot reach intent, why not let the policy \emph{judge itself}, in the lineage of self-rewarding LMs and RLAIF~\citep{selfreward,rlaif,constitutional,llmjudge}? We test this with the critic as the \emph{same model} as the policy (no larger judge), so any signal is genuine on-policy self-reflection rather than distillation, mapping outcomes across two axes: whether a verifiable rule is cheaply specifiable, and whether the on-policy critic can detect the violation (Figure~\ref{fig:selfcritique}). As a \emph{detector}, blind self-critique flags violations evident from the trajectory surface but is essentially blind to \emph{stateful-bookkeeping} norms (did it read \emph{this} file before overwriting it) even when handed the rules, and the blind spot is structural---per-rule recall stays near zero across 1.7B/4B/8B critics. As a \emph{reward} in the all-fail regime (Table~\ref{tab:sc-train}), a deterministic rule with identical credit structure is decisive on every seed, while the self-critic---\emph{with perfect recall} against the rule oracle---is inert; a \emph{frozen} critic fails identically, isolating the cause as the critic's $\sim$6\% false-positive imprecision rather than its non-stationarity. At the intent frontier (Table~\ref{tab:sc-tau2}), the self-critic is a useful \emph{offline} detector (failure-prediction F1 $0.63$ vs.\ rules $0.23$) but \emph{collapses} as a training reward. This is the criterion applied to a \emph{learned} signal: defining progress by a learned proxy relocates the credit-assignment problem into the proxy, which is then either too imprecise to optimize against or, at the intent frontier, reduces to approximating the value function itself. Verifiability is not a convenience; it is what makes the dense channel safe to optimize.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The criterion as a design tool.} The contribution is less a method than a decision procedure. Before adding a dense process reward, a practitioner can ask the three questions in turn---does a verifiable potential finer than the outcome exist; is its cheapest maximizer forced to solve the task; do base-policy rollouts reach its intermediate values---and predict the result. The five settings here were not cherry-picked successes; they were chosen to span the gates, and the criterion's verdict matched in every case, including the failures. When the answer to all three is yes, a verifiable progress credit is the right tool and the gains are large; when a penalty is the only available signal, it belongs on the \emph{harm} axis (bounding the path), never as the learning gradient in an all-fail regime; when the residual difficulty is intent, only the outcome can teach it.

\paragraph{A systems note: verifiable agentic RL is often verifier-bound.} The property that makes a process reward cheap---a machine-checkable environment---tends to make the verifier a \emph{CPU} process (a proof kernel, a test runner, a container). In our 30B theorem-proving runs the GPU generated a tactic in milliseconds and then waited seconds on the Lean kernel, leaving GPU compute utilization in the single digits at full memory. This is workload-dependent, not a law---generation-heavy or learned-verifier settings are GPU-bound---but for the verifiable domains where our criterion most often says yes, throughput is gated by environment parallelism, and the right systems design co-locates many verifier workers per accelerator rather than scaling accelerators.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The 30B un-gameability sweep is single-seed and high-variance (Figure~\ref{fig:sweep}); we therefore read its \emph{ordering and survival pattern}---which the criterion predicts and which is corroborated by the lower-variance 4B studies and the $\tau$-bench collapse---rather than its precise magnitudes, and multi-seed runs are in progress to firm the numbers. The reachability result is measured at 30B on a benchmark that is $0\%$-solve for this model; a stronger model that achieved partial fixes could make the SWE potential non-vacuous, so the claim is explicitly conditional on reachability. And our verifiable potentials are hand-identified per domain; automating their discovery is the natural next step.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Outcome RL and credit assignment.} GRPO~\citep{deepseekmath} and the R1 line~\citep{deepseekr1} established outcome-only verifiable-reward RL as the dominant recipe; the long-horizon credit-assignment difficulty has prompted step-level value estimation and turn-level shaping~\citep{gigpo,steptool}. We frame the problem as the \emph{all-fail-group blindness} of group-relative methods and characterize when a \emph{verifiable, non-learned} process signal fills the gap. DAPO~\citep{dapo} is the closest direct comparison; we show its dynamic sampling relocates the all-fail cost rather than recovering the discarded signal.

\paragraph{Process rewards, rubrics, and reward shaping.} Process reward models~\citep{prm} and step-grained tool-use rewards~\citep{steptool,toolrl} attach credit before the outcome; rubric- and verifier-based rewards~\citep{rubrics} extend verifiable RL beyond auto-checkable domains. Our criterion is orthogonal: it says \emph{when} such a dense signal is admissible, via the un-gameability/reachability conditions and the potential-based-shaping foundation~\citep{ng1999}.

\paragraph{Learned rewards and self-critique.} Self-rewarding LMs~\citep{selfreward}, RLAIF~\citep{rlaif}, Constitutional AI~\citep{constitutional}, and LLM-as-judge~\citep{llmjudge} replace rule-based reward with model judgments. Our controlled same-model study finds a learned critic dominated by a deterministic verifiable rule even at perfect recall, and collapsing where no rule applies---evidence that a learned proxy relocates rather than removes reward-hacking.

\paragraph{Demonstrations and the discovery wall.} R1-Zero~\citep{deepseekr1} self-evolves with no cold-start; off-policy guidance mixes expert trajectories into on-policy RL~\citep{luffy}. We locate the boundary empirically: reward-only suffices on discoverable workflows, and a demonstration becomes necessary precisely when a required behavior is never sampled. Customer-service benchmarks with explicit policy documents~\citep{taubench} motivate our intent-ceiling analysis.

\section{Conclusion}

Agentic RL's environment is a verifier, offering dense intermediate signal that reasoning RL never had---but using it naively either wastes it (a uniform signal centered out by group-relative RL) or is catastrophic (a gameable penalty's compliance collapse, a hacked learned critic). We gave a single criterion that resolves when the signal helps: a dense process reward improves agentic RL if and only if it is an un-gameable, verifiable potential finer than the outcome whose intermediate values the policy can reach. The criterion is checkable before training, grounded in potential-based shaping, and predictive: across five settings it called every success and every failure, including a controlled sweep in which penalty kills and outcome-gating rescues exactly as predicted. Where it says yes, the payoff is concrete---dead updates eliminated and harm cut fourfold at equal success; where it says no, it says so for a reason---intent is not a verifiable rule, and a structurally-finer potential is worthless if unreachable. The durable contribution is not another process-reward method but the line the criterion draws: between the part of agentic success that a verifiable signal can densify and accelerate, and the part that only the outcome can teach.
