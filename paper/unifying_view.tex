\subsection{A unifying account: the three gates are one variance}
\label{sec:unifying}

The same three properties decided the outcome in every setting above, which invites a single
explanation. There is one, and it is elementary; we present it not as a theorem but as the
identity that lets a reader leave with one picture instead of five.

Group-relative methods learn from a group of $G$ rollouts only through how its rewards
\emph{differ}: each reward is centred on the group mean, $A_i = R_i - \bar R$, so if the
rewards are identical every advantage is zero and the group yields no update, whatever the
rollouts did.\footnote{Standard GRPO additionally divides the advantages by the group
standard deviation; this rescales the non-zero case but does not change \emph{when} the
advantages vanish, so the reading here is unaffected.} Summarise each rollout by its outcome
plus a weighted verifiable potential, $R_i = O_i + \beta\,\Phi_i$, with $O_i\in\{0,1\}$ and
$\Phi_i$ a scalar score of the rollout's process (goals discharged, stages completed, tests
passed). The differences the group exposes are then a variance of a sum:
\[
  \operatorname{Var}_G(R)\;=\;\operatorname{Var}_G(O)\;+\;\beta^2\operatorname{Var}_G(\Phi)
  \;+\;2\beta\,\operatorname{Cov}_G(O,\Phi).
\]
The identity is trivial; what is not is that its three terms are the three gates, and that
each is what a different experiment isolates:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Outcome term -- all-fail blindness.} When every rollout fails---the common case
early in training on a hard task---$O_i\equiv 0$, so this term and the cross term drop out;
with no potential ($\beta=0$) the group is silent. This is the gap the theorem-proving
efficiency result fills (\S\ref{sec:efficiency}).
\item \textbf{Potential term -- finer and reachable.} The process channel adds signal only
when $\operatorname{Var}_G(\Phi)>0$: $\Phi$ must take more than one value (finer than the
binary outcome) \emph{and} the policy must reach those values. A potential equal to the
outcome, or one never moved off zero, contributes nothing---the software-repair null
(\S\ref{sec:law}), where $\Phi$ is $0$ on all $156$ rollouts.
\item \textbf{Cross term -- un-gameable.} A non-zero potential variance still need not point
\emph{toward} success; the covariance is what says it does. A signal whose variation is
uncorrelated with the outcome optimises something else---the compliance collapse a misaligned
penalty falls into (\S\ref{sec:law}).
\end{itemize}

We claim no more for this than it earns. It is an identity, not a proof that a process reward
helps; the cross-term reading in particular concerns training \emph{dynamics}, which only the
experiments establish, and the \emph{per-token placement} of the signal---load-bearing in
practice (\S\ref{sec:anatomy})---is invisible to this rollout-level summary, which speaks only
to whether usable group signal exists, not where it should land. What the identity buys is a
single sentence to carry into a new domain: \textbf{a useful dense process reward is the
within-group variance the outcome cannot supply}---present when that variance is non-zero
(finer, reachable) and useful when it is aligned (un-gameable). The phase diagram below puts
the sentence to work: it measures $\operatorname{Var}_G(\Phi)$ on a handful of base rollouts
and shows the benefit of a dense reward rising with exactly that quantity.
