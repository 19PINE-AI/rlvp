\begin{abstract}
The agents that will matter most act in the world on our behalf---placing phone calls, navigating menus, resolving support tickets---where every rollout is a real, slow, often irreversible interaction rather than a step in a cheap simulator. Two things follow. First, whether such an agent is \emph{deployable} is decided by its path, not its outcome: it must not keep calling a user who has not answered, must not act outside business hours, must obtain a service's required authentication before it proceeds. These are constraints the outcome reward cannot express---doing more of the forbidden thing often \emph{improves} the outcome. Second, because interactions are expensive, the agent must learn from few of them; the brute-force trial-and-error that math and code afford in simulation is unavailable.

Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) optimizes the one signal an environment checks cheaply---the outcome---and is blind to both needs: it cannot see the path, and it wastes its costly rollouts on the all-failing groups where a group-relative advantage is exactly zero. The instinct to go denser is to reward \emph{progress}, but that is the hard-to-verify direction. Agentic environments are asymmetric verifiers: they cheaply confirm a \emph{bad} move (a call before its precondition, an action out of hours) but not that the agent is on the right track.

We make this precise with a within-group-variance account: a group-relative advantage is a within-group variance, so a dense signal helps only where it supplies the variance the outcome lacks, and only where the policy reaches the states that carry it. A verifiable \emph{penalty} on the path meets this by construction; a dense progress \emph{potential} meets it only where partial progress is reachable, and there it converts the costly all-fail rollouts into learning. This yields one recipe---\textbf{penalize the path, reward the outcome}---and its mirror, densifying the sparse outcome with a verifiable potential where progress is reachable. On tractable proxies for the deployment setting, the penalty attains the task with near-zero violations where outcome-only training violates every episode and cuts harmful actions \emph{sixfold} at equal success; the potential removes the dead all-fail updates that dominate expensive early training. We give four design rules for verifiable penalties---including that a lone penalty stalls in an \emph{inaction trap}---and locate exactly where each half of the signal helps.
\end{abstract}
